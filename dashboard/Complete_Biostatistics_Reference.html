Probability Distributions for Count Data

Spatial transcriptomics data consists of counts — the number of detected mRNA transcripts per gene per cell. The choice of distributional model fundamentally affects all downstream inference.

1.1 The Poisson Distribution

Definition 1.1 (Poisson Distribution)
A random variable $Y$ follows a Poisson distribution with rate parameter $\lambda > 0$, written $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, if: $$P(Y = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \dots$$

Properties:

  • Mean: $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \lambda$
  • Variance: $\text{Var}(Y) = \lambda$  (mean equals variance)
  • Mode: $\lfloor\lambda\rfloor$  (greatest integer $\leq \lambda$)
  • Moment generating function: $M_Y(t) = \exp(\lambda(e^t - 1))$
  • Cumulant generating function: $K_Y(t) = \lambda(e^t - 1)$, so all cumulants equal $\lambda$
  • Additivity: If $Y_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ and $Y_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ are independent, then $Y_1 + Y_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$
  • Thinning: If $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and each event is independently retained with probability $\delta$, the retained count follows $\text{Poisson}(\delta\lambda)$
Example 1.1 — Transcript Detection

A cell expresses gene EGFR at rate $\lambda = 8$ transcripts. The platform has detection efficiency $\delta = 0.6$. The observed count is:

$$Y \sim \text{Poisson}(0.6 \times 8) = \text{Poisson}(4.8)$$

Expected count: 4.8. Standard deviation: $\sqrt{4.8} \approx 2.19$. Relative noise: $2.19/4.8 \approx 46\%$.

Probability of observing exactly 3 transcripts:

$$P(Y=3) = \frac{4.8^3 \, e^{-4.8}}{3!} = \frac{110.59 \times 0.00823}{6} = 0.152$$

1.2 The Negative Binomial Distribution

Definition 1.2 (Negative Binomial Distribution)
$Y \sim \text{NB}(\mu, \theta)$ where $\mu > 0$ is the mean and $\theta > 0$ is the dispersion parameter: $$P(Y=k) = \binom{k + \theta - 1}{k} \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta + \mu}\right)^\theta \left(\frac{\mu}{\theta + \mu}\right)^k$$

Properties:

  • Mean: $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mu$
  • Variance: $\text{Var}(Y) = \mu + \mu^2/\theta$  (variance > mean, i.e., overdispersion)
  • Limit: As $\theta \to \infty$, $\text{NB}(\mu, \theta) \to \text{Poisson}(\mu)$
Remark 1.1 — Poisson vs. Negative Binomial for Spatial Omics

The Poisson model assumes variance equals mean (technical noise only). The Negative Binomial allows overdispersion (biological variability between cells of the same type). For imaging-based spatial transcriptomics (Xenium, MERFISH, CosMx), individual molecule detection is well-modeled by Poisson. For sequencing-based platforms (Visium), the NB model is more appropriate due to PCR amplification noise.

In this framework, we use the Poisson model because our primary data type (Xenium) measures individual molecules without amplification. The extension to NB is straightforward by replacing $\text{Var}(Y) = \lambda$ with $\text{Var}(Y) = \lambda + \lambda^2/\theta$.

1.3 The Zero-Inflated Poisson (ZIP) Model

Definition 1.3 (Zero-Inflated Poisson)
$Y \sim \text{ZIP}(\lambda, \pi)$ is a mixture: $$P(Y=k) = \begin{cases} \pi + (1-\pi)e^{-\lambda} & k=0 \\ (1-\pi)\frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} & k \geq 1 \end{cases}$$ where $\pi \in [0,1)$ is the zero-inflation probability (probability of a "structural" zero — the gene is truly silent in that cell, not just undetected).

Properties:

  • $\mathbb{E}[Y] = (1-\pi)\lambda$
  • $\text{Var}(Y) = (1-\pi)\lambda(1 + \pi\lambda)$
Critical Issue: Biological vs. Technical Zeros

Hafiz et al. (Briefings in Bioinformatics, 2026) showed that all tested imputation methods overimpute — they fill in both technical dropouts (good) and biological zeros (bad), destroying biological signal. This makes the distinction between $\pi$ (biological silence) and detection failure ($\delta_g$) critical for reliable downstream analysis.

1.4 The Multinomial and Dirichlet-Multinomial

For compositional analysis (e.g., cell type proportions in a spatial spot):

Definition 1.4 (Multinomial Distribution)
$\mathbf{Y} = (Y_1, \dots, Y_T) \sim \text{Multinomial}(n, \mathbf{p})$ where $n$ is total count and $\mathbf{p} = (p_1, \dots, p_T)$ with $\sum_t p_t = 1$: $$P(\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{y}) = \frac{n!}{\prod_t y_t!} \prod_t p_t^{y_t}$$

The Dirichlet-Multinomial adds cell type proportion uncertainty: $\mathbf{p} \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$, then $\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{p} \sim \text{Multinomial}(n, \mathbf{p})$.

The Poisson Measurement Model for Spatial Omics

2.1 Model Specification

The Complete Observation Model

For cell $i \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ at spatial position $\mathbf{s}_i \in \mathbb{R}^2$ and gene $g \in \{1, \dots, G\}$:

$$y_{ig} \sim \text{Poisson}(\delta_g \cdot \lambda_{ig}), \quad g \in S$$ $$y_{ig} = \text{unobserved}, \quad g \notin S$$

where:

  • $y_{ig} \in \mathbb{Z}_+ = \{0, 1, 2, \dots\}$ is the observed transcript count
  • $\lambda_{ig} > 0$ is the true biological expression rate
  • $\delta_g \in (0, 1]$ is the gene-specific detection efficiency
  • $S \subset \{1, \dots, G\}$ is the measured gene panel with $|S| = m \ll G$

2.2 The Selection Matrix Formulation

Define the selection matrix $A_S \in \{0,1\}^{m \times G}$:

$$(A_S)_{jg} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if gene } g \text{ is the } j\text{-th element of panel } S \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

$A_S$ consists of $m$ rows of the $G \times G$ identity matrix. The observed data for cell $i$:

$$\mathbf{y}_{i,\text{obs}} = A_S \, \mathbf{y}_i \in \mathbb{Z}_+^m$$

with each component satisfying:

$$[A_S \mathbf{y}_i]_j \sim \text{Poisson}([A_S \, \boldsymbol{\delta} \odot \boldsymbol{\lambda}_i]_j)$$

where $\odot$ is the Hadamard (elementwise) product.

2.3 Signal-to-Noise Analysis

SNR Decomposition

The signal-to-noise ratio for gene $g$ in cell $i$:

$$\text{SNR}_{ig} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[y_{ig}]^2}{\text{Var}(y_{ig})} = \frac{(\delta_g \lambda_{ig})^2}{\delta_g \lambda_{ig}} = \delta_g \lambda_{ig}$$

The coefficient of variation:

$$\text{CV}_{ig} = \frac{\sqrt{\text{Var}(y_{ig})}}{\mathbb{E}[y_{ig}]} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\delta_g \lambda_{ig}}}$$

$$\mathbf{Y} \in \mathbb{Z}_+^{N \times G} \quad \text{(full count matrix — mostly unobserved)}$$ $$\mathbf{Y}_{\text{obs}} = \mathbf{Y} A_S^\top \in \mathbb{Z}_+^{N \times m} \quad \text{(observed data)}$$ $$\boldsymbol{\Lambda} \in \mathbb{R}_+^{N \times G} \quad \text{(true rate matrix — the target)}$$

2.5 Log-Likelihood Function

The joint log-likelihood of all observed data, given the rate matrix $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$:

Poisson Log-Likelihood
$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\Lambda}; \mathbf{Y}_{\text{obs}}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{g \in S} \left[ y_{ig} \log(\delta_g \lambda_{ig}) - \delta_g \lambda_{ig} - \log(y_{ig}!) \right]$$

Dropping constant terms and absorbing $\delta_g$ into $\lambda_{ig}$:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\Lambda}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{g \in S} \left[ y_{ig} \log \lambda_{ig} - \lambda_{ig} \right]$$

Low-Rank Matrix Structure and Gene Programs

3.1 The Biological Basis for Low Rank

Gene expression is not arbitrary. Cells activate coordinated gene programs — sets of genes regulated together by shared transcription factors, signaling pathways, or epigenetic states. For example:

  • Proliferation program: MKI67, TOP2A, CDK1, PCNA co-express
  • Interferon response: ISG15, MX1, IFIT1, STAT1 co-express
  • EMT program: VIM, FN1, SNAI1/2, ZEB1 co-express

If there are $K$ independent programs, the expression of all $G$ genes is determined by $K$ latent factors — the rate matrix $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is approximately rank-$K$.

3.2 The Log-Linear Factor Model

Gene Program Decomposition
$$\log \boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \mathbf{1}\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top + \mathbf{U}\mathbf{V}^\top + \mathbf{E}$$

Elementwise:

$$\log \lambda_{ig} = \mu_g + \sum_{k=1}^{K} u_{ik} v_{gk} + \epsilon_{ig}$$
SymbolDimensionInterpretationWhat Determines It
$\mu_g$scalar per geneBaseline expression level of gene $g$Gene identity (housekeeping vs. rare)
$u_{ik}$scalar per cell-program pairActivity of program $k$ in cell $i$Cell type + cell state
$v_{gk}$scalar per gene-program pairMembership of gene $g$ in program $k$Gene regulatory network
$\epsilon_{ig}$scalar per cell-gene pairResidual noiseStochastic gene expression

3.3 Identifiability and Parameter Counting

Total unknowns in the factor model (ignoring $\mathbf{E}$):

$$\text{Parameters} = \underbrace{G}_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} + \underbrace{NK}_{\mathbf{U}} + \underbrace{GK}_{\mathbf{V}} = G(1+K) + NK$$

Observations: $Nm$ (one count per cell per measured gene).

Proposition 3.1 (Necessary Condition for Identifiability)

Recovery of $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ from $\mathbf{Y}_{\text{obs}}$ requires at minimum:

$$Nm \geq G(1+K) + NK$$ $$\Leftrightarrow \quad m \geq \frac{G(1+K)}{N} + K$$

For $N \gg G$: $m \gtrsim K$.

For $N = G$: $m \gtrsim 2K + 1$.

3.4 Empirical Rank Estimation

To estimate $K$ empirically from data $\mathbf{Y}_{\text{obs}}$:

  1. Normalize: $\tilde{\mathbf{Y}} = \log(1 + \text{CP10K}(\mathbf{Y}_{\text{obs}}))$
  2. Center: $\bar{\mathbf{Y}} = \tilde{\mathbf{Y}} - \mathbf{1}\bar{\mathbf{y}}^\top$
  3. Compute SVD: $\bar{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top$
  4. $K$ = number of singular values $\sigma_k$ exceeding the Marchenko-Pastur threshold (random matrix theory baseline)

The Marchenko-Pastur law gives the expected largest singular value of a random $N \times m$ matrix with i.i.d. entries of variance $\sigma^2$:

$$\sigma_{\text{MP}} = \sigma \left(1 + \sqrt{N/m}\right)$$

Singular values exceeding this are signal; those below are noise.

Matrix Completion and Recovery Theory

4.1 The Recovery Problem

Given observed entries $\{y_{ig} : g \in S\}$ and the low-rank model, we seek to recover the full rate matrix $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} \in \mathbb{R}_+^{N \times G}$.

Penalized Poisson Maximum Likelihood
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\Lambda}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\Lambda} \succ 0} \left[ -\ell(\boldsymbol{\Lambda}) + \tau \|\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\|_* \right]$$

where $\|\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\|_* = \sum_k \sigma_k(\boldsymbol{\Lambda})$ is the nuclear norm (convex relaxation of rank), and $\tau > 0$ is the regularization strength.

4.2 The Incoherence Condition

Definition 4.1 (Incoherence)

A rank-$K$ matrix $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times G}$ with SVD $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top$ is $\mu_0$-incoherent if:

$$\max_i \|\mathbf{U}_{i,:}\|^2 \leq \frac{\mu_0 K}{N}, \qquad \max_g \|\mathbf{V}_{g,:}\|^2 \leq \frac{\mu_0 K}{G}$$

This requires that no single cell or gene dominates the signal. $\mu_0 = 1$ is the best case (uniform spread); $\mu_0 = N/K$ is the worst (all signal in one cell).

Biological interpretation: Incoherence fails when one cell type is extremely rare (all signal in a few cells) or one gene uniquely defines a program (all signal in one gene). This predicts that rare cell types are harder to recover — a biologically meaningful limitation.

4.3 Recovery Guarantee

Theorem 4.1 (Poisson Matrix Completion; after Cao & Xie, 2015)

Let $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} \in \mathbb{R}_+^{N \times G}$ have rank $K$, be $\mu_0$-incoherent, with $\lambda_{\max} = \max_{i,g} \lambda_{ig}$. If the panel $S$ is chosen uniformly at random with $|S| = m$, and:

$$m \geq C_0 \mu_0^2 K^2 \log^2(N+G)$$

then with probability at least $1 - (N+G)^{-3}$:

$$\frac{1}{NG}\|\hat{\boldsymbol{\Lambda}} - \boldsymbol{\Lambda}\|_F^2 \leq C_1 \cdot \frac{K(N+G)\lambda_{\max}}{Nm}$$

For $N \gg G$, this simplifies to:

$$\boxed{\text{MSE} \approx C \cdot \frac{K \lambda_{\max}}{m}}$$

4.4 Understanding the Bound

FactorEffect on MSEWhy

$m$ increasesMSE decreases as $1/m$More observed genes provide more constraints

4.5 The Factored Estimator

In practice, we solve the factored form (computationally more efficient than nuclear norm minimization):

Factored Poisson NMF
$$\hat{\mathbf{U}}, \hat{\mathbf{V}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{U}, \mathbf{V}} \sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{g \in S} \left[\exp\!\left(\mu_g + \mathbf{u}_i^\top \mathbf{v}_g\right) - y_{ig}\left(\mu_g + \mathbf{u}_i^\top \mathbf{v}_g\right)\right] + \frac{\tau}{2}\left(\|\mathbf{U}\|_F^2 + \|\mathbf{V}\|_F^2\right)$$

The regularization $\|\mathbf{U}\|_F^2 + \|\mathbf{V}\|_F^2$ implicitly constrains the nuclear norm of $\mathbf{U}\mathbf{V}^\top$.

Per-Gene Reconstruction Error Analysis

5.1 MSE Decomposition

The reconstruction error for gene $g$ can be decomposed into two independent sources:

Per-Gene MSE
$$\text{MSE}(g) = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\delta_g \bar{\lambda}_g}}_{\substack{\text{Poisson shot noise}\\\text{(irreducible)}}} + \underbrace{\|\mathbf{v}_g\|^2 (1 - R_g^2)}_{\substack{\text{Panel incompleteness}\\\text{(reducible by panel design)}}}$$

where $\bar{\lambda}_g = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \lambda_{ig}$ is the mean expression of gene $g$.

5.2 Gene Predictability: The $R^2_g$ Statistic

Definition 5.1 (Latent-Space $R^2$)

The predictability of unmeasured gene $g$ from panel $S$, in the latent factor space:

$$R_g^2 = \frac{\mathbf{v}_g^\top \mathbf{P}_{V_S} \mathbf{v}_g}{\|\mathbf{v}_g\|^2}$$

where $\mathbf{P}_{V_S} = \mathbf{V}_S^\top(\mathbf{V}_S \mathbf{V}_S^\top)^{-1}\mathbf{V}_S$ is the projection matrix onto the column space of $\mathbf{V}_S^\top$ (the latent representations of the measured genes).

Derivation: The expression of gene $g$ in the latent space is $\mathbf{v}_g \in \mathbb{R}^K$. The best linear predictor of $\mathbf{v}_g$ from the measured gene loadings $\mathbf{V}_S = [\mathbf{v}_{g'}]_{g' \in S}$ is the orthogonal projection $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_g = \mathbf{P}_{V_S}\mathbf{v}_g$. The fraction of variance explained is $R_g^2$.

Residual variance:

$$\sigma_g^2 = \|\mathbf{v}_g - \hat{\mathbf{v}}_g\|^2 = \|\mathbf{v}_g\|^2 - \mathbf{v}_g^\top \mathbf{P}_{V_S} \mathbf{v}_g = \|\mathbf{v}_g\|^2(1 - R_g^2)$$
Example 5.1 — Gene Predictability

Suppose $K = 3$ programs. Gene CXCL12 has loading $\mathbf{v}_{\text{CXCL12}} = (0.8, 0.3, 0.1)$ (primarily in program 1). If the panel $S$ contains genes that span programs 1 and 2 but not 3:

  • The projection captures the (0.8, 0.3) components but misses 0.1
  • $R^2_{\text{CXCL12}} = (0.8^2 + 0.3^2)/(0.8^2 + 0.3^2 + 0.1^2) = 0.73/0.74 = 0.986$
  • Residual variance: $0.74 \times (1 - 0.986) = 0.010$
  • This gene is highly predictable — imputation will work well.

Now consider gene FOXP3 with loading $\mathbf{v}_{\text{FOXP3}} = (0.05, 0.05, 0.9)$ (primarily in the uncovered program 3):

  • $R^2_{\text{FOXP3}} = (0.05^2 + 0.05^2)/(0.05^2 + 0.05^2 + 0.9^2) = 0.005/0.815 = 0.006$
  • Residual variance: $0.815 \times (1 - 0.006) = 0.810$
  • This gene is essentially unrecoverable from this panel.

Spatial Autocorrelation: Moran's I

6.1 Definition and Formula

Moran's I (Global Spatial Autocorrelation)

Question: "Are nearby cells more similar in expression than expected by chance?"

$$I = \frac{N}{W} \cdot \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N w_{ij}(x_i - \bar{x})(x_j - \bar{x})}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \bar{x})^2}$$

Equivalently, using matrix notation with $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \dots, x_N)^\top$, centered $\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x} - \bar{x}\mathbf{1}$:

$$I = \frac{N}{W} \cdot \frac{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^\top \mathbf{W} \tilde{\mathbf{x}}}{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}}$$

where $\mathbf{W}$ is the spatial weight matrix with $W_{ij} = w_{ij}$ and $W = \sum_{ij} w_{ij}$.

6.2 Expected Value and Variance Under the Null

Proposition 6.1 (Null Distribution of Moran's I)

Under the null hypothesis of no spatial autocorrelation (random permutation of values across locations):

$$\mathbb{E}[I] = -\frac{1}{N-1}$$ $$\text{Var}(I) = \frac{N^2 S_1 - N S_2 + 3W^2}{W^2(N^2 - 1)} - \left(\frac{1}{N-1}\right)^2$$

where $S_1 = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i\sum_j (w_{ij} + w_{ji})^2$ and $S_2 = \sum_i (w_{i\cdot} + w_{\cdot i})^2$.

The Z-score for testing spatial autocorrelation:

$$Z_I = \frac{I - \mathbb{E}[I]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(I)}}$$

Under the null, $Z_I \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ for large $N$.

6.5 Local Moran's I (LISA)

Definition 6.1 (Local Indicator of Spatial Association)
$$I_i = \frac{(x_i - \bar{x})}{\frac{1}{N}\sum_j (x_j - \bar{x})^2} \sum_j w_{ij}(x_j - \bar{x})$$

Properties: $\sum_i I_i = N \cdot I$ (local values sum to global). Local Moran's $I_i$ identifies individual cells contributing to spatial autocorrelation: HH (hot spot), LL (cold spot), HL (spatial outlier high), LH (spatial outlier low).

Spatial Autocorrelation: Geary's C

7.1 Definition and Formula

Geary's C (Local Dissimilarity Measure)

Question: "How different are neighboring cells from each other?"

$$C = \frac{(N-1)}{2W} \cdot \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N w_{ij}(x_i - x_j)^2}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \bar{x})^2}$$

7.2 Relationship to Moran's I

Expanding $(x_i - x_j)^2 = (x_i - \bar{x})^2 - 2(x_i-\bar{x})(x_j-\bar{x}) + (x_j - \bar{x})^2$:

$$C = \frac{(N-1)}{2W} \cdot \frac{\sum_{ij} w_{ij}\left[(x_i-\bar{x})^2 + (x_j-\bar{x})^2\right] - 2\sum_{ij}w_{ij}(x_i-\bar{x})(x_j-\bar{x})}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar{x})^2}$$

For a symmetric weight matrix with constant row sums (regular lattice): $C \approx 1 - I$. In general, $C$ and $I$ are inversely but not perfectly linearly related.

7.3 Null Distribution

Proposition 7.1

Under random permutation:

$$\mathbb{E}[C] = 1, \qquad Z_C = \frac{C - 1}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(C)}}$$

Key observation: The 4 boundary edges (3↔6, 4↔7, 5↔6, 5↔8) contribute $49+36+36+36 = 157$ out of 168 total — 93.5% of the signal. Geary's C is dominated by the boundary between high and low expression regions.

Neighborhood Enrichment (NE) Score

8.1 Definition

Neighborhood Enrichment Score

Question: "Do cells of type $a$ neighbor cells of type $b$ more (or less) often than expected by chance?"

For cell types $a$ and $b$:

$$NE(a,b) = \frac{n_{\text{obs}}(a,b) - \mu_{\text{perm}}(a,b)}{\sigma_{\text{perm}}(a,b)}$$

where:

  • $n_{\text{obs}}(a,b) = \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}} \mathbb{1}[c_i = a, c_j = b]$  (observed count of $a$-$b$ neighbor pairs)
  • $\mu_{\text{perm}}(a,b) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[n(a,b)]$  (expected count under random label permutations)
  • $\sigma_{\text{perm}}(a,b) = \text{Std}_\pi[n(a,b)]$  (standard deviation under permutations)

8.2 Analytical Expected Value

Under random permutation of cell type labels, the expected number of $a$-$b$ edges:

$$\mu_{\text{perm}}(a,b) = |\mathcal{E}| \cdot \begin{cases} \frac{n_a(n_a-1)}{N(N-1)} & \text{if } a = b \\[6pt] \frac{2 \, n_a \, n_b}{N(N-1)} & \text{if } a \neq b \end{cases}$$

where $n_a = |\{i : c_i = a\}|$ is the number of cells of type $a$, and $|\mathcal{E}|$ is the total number of directed edges.

8.4 NE Score Matrix

For $T$ cell types, the full NE matrix is $T \times T$:

$$\mathbf{NE} = \begin{pmatrix} NE(1,1) & NE(1,2) & \cdots & NE(1,T) \\ NE(2,1) & NE(2,2) & \cdots & NE(2,T) \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\ NE(T,1) & NE(T,2) & \cdots & NE(T,T) \end{pmatrix}$$

Diagonal entries: self-clustering. Off-diagonal: cross-type interactions. This matrix is the primary output of squidpy's sq.gr.nhood_enrichment().

Cell-Cell Interaction (CCI) Test Statistic

9.1 The Ligand-Receptor Co-Expression Score

CCI Test Statistic

For ligand gene $l$ and receptor gene $r$, the spatial co-expression score:

$$T_{lr} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|} \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}} x_{il} \cdot x_{jr}$$

where $\mathcal{E} = \{(i,j) : w_{ij} = 1\}$ is the set of spatial neighbor pairs.

Biological interpretation: $T_{lr}$ measures whether cells expressing ligand $l$ tend to be spatially adjacent to cells expressing receptor $r$. High $T_{lr}$ suggests a potential signaling interaction.

9.2 Variants

9.3 Null Model and Expected Value

Under the null of no spatial interaction (expression and location are independent):

$$\mathbb{E}_0[T_{lr}] = \bar{x}_l \cdot \bar{x}_r + \frac{I_W}{N-1}(\bar{x^2}_l - \bar{x}_l^2)^{1/2}(\bar{x^2}_r - \bar{x}_r^2)^{1/2}$$

where $I_W$ is the Moran-like autocorrelation of the spatial weight matrix. For most spatial graphs, $I_W / (N-1)$ is negligible, so:

$$\mathbb{E}_0[T_{lr}] \approx \bar{x}_l \cdot \bar{x}_r$$

Permutation Testing and Multiple Comparison Correction

10.1 Permutation Test for CCI

Algorithm: Permutation Test for CCI Significance
  1. Compute observed statistic: $T_{lr}^{\text{obs}}$
  2. For $b = 1, \dots, B$:
    • Draw random permutation $\pi_b$ of cell indices
    • Compute null statistic: $T_{lr}^{(b)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}} x_{\pi_b(i), l} \cdot x_{j,r}$
  3. $p$-value: $p = \frac{\#\{b : T_{lr}^{(b)} \geq T_{lr}^{\text{obs}}\} + 1}{B + 1}$

The "+1" in numerator and denominator ensures the $p$-value is never exactly 0 (conservative correction).

10.2 Benjamini-Hochberg FDR Control

Definition 10.1 (False Discovery Rate)
$$\text{FDR} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{V}{R \vee 1}\right]$$

where $V$ = number of false rejections (false positives), $R$ = total rejections, and $a \vee b = \max(a,b)$.

Benjamini-Hochberg Procedure

Given $L$ tests with $p$-values $p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq p_{(L)}$:

  1. Find the largest $k$ such that $p_{(k)} \leq \frac{k}{L} \cdot q$, where $q$ is the target FDR (typically 0.05)
  2. Reject all hypotheses $H_{(1)}, \dots, H_{(k)}$

Adjusted $p$-values: $p_{(i)}^{\text{BH}} = \min\left\{\frac{L}{i} p_{(i)}, \; 1\right\}$ (with monotonicity enforcement).

10.3 Impact of Multiple Testing on CCI

Example 10.1

Testing 200 LR pairs at FDR $q = 0.05$. If 40 have true signal and 160 are null:

  • Expected false discoveries at 5% FDR: $0.05 \times R \approx 0.05 \times 50 = 2.5$
  • But if imputation inflates scores, many null pairs cross the significance threshold
  • BH procedure assumes valid $p$-values — biased test statistics produce anti-conservative $p$-values, and BH no longer controls FDR at the nominal level

This is why regression calibration (Section 12) is essential: it restores validity of the $p$-values.

Bias Decomposition Under Measurement Error

11.1 The Measurement Error Model

After imputation, the estimated expression for cell $i$, gene $g$ is:

$$\hat{\lambda}_{ig} = \lambda_{ig} + e_{ig}$$

where $e_{ig}$ is the imputation error with:

  • $\mathbb{E}[e_{ig}] \approx 0$  (unbiased imputation, approximately)
  • $\text{Var}(e_{ig}) = \text{MSE}(g)$  (gene-specific error variance)
  • $\text{Cov}(e_{ig}, e_{jg}) \neq 0$ when $w_{ij} = 1$  (spatially correlated errors)

11.2 Bias in the CCI Statistic

Bias Derivation

The CCI statistic computed on imputed data:

$$\hat{T}_{lr} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{E}} \hat{\lambda}_{il} \cdot \hat{\lambda}_{jr}$$

Substituting $\hat{\lambda}_{il} = \lambda_{il} + e_{il}$ and $\hat{\lambda}_{jr} = \lambda_{jr} + e_{jr}$:

\begin{align} \hat{\lambda}_{il} \cdot \hat{\lambda}_{jr} &= (\lambda_{il} + e_{il})(\lambda_{jr} + e_{jr}) \\ &= \lambda_{il}\lambda_{jr} + \lambda_{il} e_{jr} + \lambda_{jr} e_{il} + e_{il} e_{jr} \end{align}

Taking expectations:

\begin{align} \mathbb{E}[\hat{T}_{lr}] &= \underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)} \lambda_{il}\lambda_{jr}}_{= T_{lr}^{\text{true}}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)} \lambda_{il}\mathbb{E}[e_{jr}]}_{= 0} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)} \lambda_{jr}\mathbb{E}[e_{il}]}_{= 0} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)} \mathbb{E}[e_{il} e_{jr}]}_{= \text{BIAS}} \end{align}

11.3 Why the Bias Is Positive for Graph-Based Methods

The bias term is:

$$\text{bias} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}} \text{Cov}(e_{il}, e_{jr})$$
The Fundamental Problem

Graph-based imputation methods (GNN, graph diffusion, spatial smoothing) share information across spatial neighbors. If cell $j$ is a neighbor of cell $i$, the imputed value $\hat{\lambda}_{jg}$ depends partly on $\hat{\lambda}_{ig}$ (through message passing). This means:

$$\text{Cov}(e_{il}, e_{jr}) > 0 \quad \text{when } (i,j) \in \mathcal{E}$$

But $\mathcal{E}$ is the exact same set of edges used in the CCI computation. The imputation method creates positive correlations on the same edges where we test for biological interactions. The bias is structurally guaranteed to be positive.

11.4 Magnitude Estimation

Under the approximation that error correlations are proportional to Moran's I:

$$\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}} \text{Cov}(e_{il}, e_{jr}) \approx \rho_{lr} \cdot \sqrt{\text{MSE}(l) \cdot \text{MSE}(r)}$$

where $\rho_{lr} = \sqrt{|I_l| \cdot |I_r|}$ is the cross-gene spatial error correlation and $I_l, I_r$ are Moran's I values of the imputed expression for the ligand and receptor genes.

Regression Calibration for CCI Correction

Regression calibration (Carroll, Ruppert, Stefanski & Crainiceanu, 2006) is a standard technique for correcting bias in statistics computed from error-contaminated variables.

12.1 The Correction

Regression-Calibrated CCI Statistic
$$\hat{T}_{lr}^{\text{corrected}} = \hat{T}_{lr}^{\text{naive}} - \widehat{\text{bias}}_{lr}$$

where:

$$\widehat{\text{bias}}_{lr} = \hat{\rho}_{lr} \cdot \sqrt{\widehat{\text{MSE}}(l) \cdot \widehat{\text{MSE}}(r)}$$ $$\hat{\rho}_{lr} = \sqrt{|I_l| \cdot |I_r|}$$

12.2 MSE Estimation via Cross-Validation

For measured genes ($g \in S$): direct comparison with observed counts.

$$\widehat{\text{MSE}}(g) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (x_{ig}^{\text{norm}} - \hat{\lambda}_{ig})^2, \quad g \in S$$

For unmeasured genes ($g \notin S$): predict MSE from a meta-model trained on measured genes. Features: mean expression, variance, latent-space $R^2_g$.

$$\widehat{\text{MSE}}(g) = f_{\text{meta}}(\bar{\hat{\lambda}}_g, \; \text{Var}(\hat{\lambda}_{ig}), \; R_g^2), \quad g \notin S$$

12.3 Corrected Variance and P-Values

Corrected Inference

The variance of the corrected statistic includes uncertainty from the correction itself:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{T}_{lr}^{\text{corr}}) \approx \text{Var}(\hat{T}_{lr}^{\text{naive}}) + \frac{\widehat{\text{MSE}}(l) \cdot \widehat{\text{MSE}}(r)}{|\mathcal{E}|}$$

The corrected permutation test subtracts the same bias estimate from null replicates:

$$T_{lr}^{(b),\text{corr}} = T_{lr}^{(b),\text{naive}} - \widehat{\text{bias}}_{lr}$$ $$p^{\text{corr}} = \frac{\#\{b : T_{lr}^{(b),\text{corr}} \geq \hat{T}_{lr}^{\text{corr}}\} + 1}{B+1}$$

Error Propagation to Cell Type Classification

13.1 Nearest-Centroid Classifier

Cell type classification assigns cell $i$ to the type whose centroid is closest in expression space:

$$\hat{c}_i = \arg\min_t \|\hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)}\|_2$$

13.2 Misclassification Bound

Theorem 13.1 (Error Propagation to Cell Typing)

For a cell of true type $t$, the misclassification probability satisfies:

$$P(\hat{c}_i \neq t) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta_t^2}{8 \, \sigma_{\text{recon}}^2}\right)$$

where:

$$\Delta_t = \min_{t' \neq t} \|\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t')}\|_2$$ $$\sigma_{\text{recon}}^2 = \sum_{g=1}^G \text{MSE}(g)$$

Derivation sketch: Misclassification occurs when $\|\hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t')}\| < \|\hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)}\|$ for some $t' \neq t$. This requires the error $\mathbf{e}_i = \hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_i - \boldsymbol{\lambda}_i$ to have a component of magnitude $\geq \Delta_t/2$ along the direction $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t')}$. By the sub-Gaussian tail bound on the projected error: $P(\langle \mathbf{e}_i, \mathbf{d}_{tt'}\rangle > \Delta_t/2) \leq \exp(-\Delta_t^2 / (8\sigma_{\text{recon}}^2))$.

13.3 Critical Panel Size per Cell Type

Setting the misclassification probability to a target $\alpha$:

$$m_t^* = \frac{8 \, C \, K \, \lambda_{\max}}{\Delta_t^2 \cdot \log(1/\alpha)}$$

When both ligand and receptor are imputed, the variance of the CCI test statistic grows as the product of their individual MSEs. This means CCI reliability degrades quadratically — far faster than gene-level imputation quality alone would suggest.

Error Propagation to Spatial Domain Detection

Spatial domain detection clusters cells based on expression similarity + spatial proximity. The effective similarity between neighboring cells:

$$w_{ij}^{\text{eff}} = \text{sim}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_i, \hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_j) \cdot f(\|\mathbf{s}_i - \mathbf{s}_j\|)$$

Reconstruction error perturbs similarity:

$$\text{sim}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_i, \hat{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}_j) \approx \text{sim}(\boldsymbol{\lambda}_i, \boldsymbol{\lambda}_j) + O\!\left(\frac{\sigma_{\text{recon}}}{\Delta_{\min}}\right)$$

Domains merge (under-segmentation) or split (over-segmentation) when $\sigma_{\text{recon}} / \Delta_{\min} \gtrsim 1$, where $\Delta_{\min}$ is the minimum inter-domain expression distance.

Panel Perturbation Stability Analysis

16.1 Procedure

Panel Perturbation Algorithm

Input: Panel $S$ ($m$ genes), imputation function $f$, downstream analysis $\phi$.

  1. For $b = 1, \dots, B$:
    • Sample sub-panel $S_b^* \subset S$ by dropping fraction $\alpha$ of genes uniformly at random
    • Re-impute: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Lambda}}_b = f(\mathbf{Y}_{\text{obs}}, S_b^*)$
    • Re-run downstream: $\theta_b = \phi(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Lambda}}_b)$  (e.g., CCI score for each LR pair)
  2. Compute stability metrics from $\{\theta_1, \dots, \theta_B\}$

16.2 Stability Metrics

MetricFormulaInterpretation
Coefficient of Variation$\text{CV} = \text{Std}(\theta_b) / |\text{Mean}(\theta_b)|$Relative variability of the conclusion

16.3 Why This Is Not a Bootstrap

Remark 16.1 — Methodological Honesty

A bootstrap resamples observations (cells) and provides coverage guarantees under regularity conditions. Our procedure resamples features (genes) — a fundamentally different operation. We do not claim that the perturbation intervals have nominal coverage. Instead, we claim:

  1. Stability (low CV across sub-panels) predicts actual reliability
  2. Instability (high CV) is a diagnostic flag for unreliable conclusions

This must be validated empirically by showing correlation between predicted stability and actual error (ground truth comparison).

Calibration and Discrimination Metrics

17.1 Discrimination (Ranking Quality)

Can we rank LR pairs from most to least reliable?

$$r_s = \text{Spearman}(\text{Stability}_{lr}, \; |\hat{T}_{lr} - T_{lr}^{\text{true}}|)$$

Target: $|r_s| \geq 0.7$. A negative $r_s$ means high stability predicts low error (desired).

17.2 Calibration (Absolute Accuracy)

Does predicted reliability match actual false discovery proportion?

Calibration Plot Construction
  1. Restrict to LR pairs called significant in imputed data
  2. Bin by predicted reliability (quintiles)
  3. In each bin $b$, compute: $$\text{FDP}(b) = 1 - \frac{|\{(l,r) \in b : (l,r) \in \text{True Significant}\}|}{|\{(l,r) \in b\}|}$$
  4. Plot FDP vs. mean reliability per bin

Perfect calibration: FDP decreases monotonically as reliability increases. Highest reliability bin should have FDP $< 0.1$; lowest reliability bin should have FDP $> 0.5$.

17.3 Expected Calibration Error (ECE)

$$\text{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{|n_b|}{n} \left| \text{FDP}(b) - (1 - \overline{\text{Stab}}(b)) \right|$$

where $n_b$ is the number of pairs in bin $b$ and $\overline{\text{Stab}}(b)$ is the mean stability in bin $b$. Lower ECE = better calibration.

Gene Panel Optimization Theory

18.1 Optimal Panel Selection as Subset Selection

Given the framework, the optimal panel $S^*$ minimizes expected downstream error:

$$S^* = \arg\min_{S \subset \{1,\dots,G\}, |S|=m} \sum_{(l,r) \in \mathcal{L}} \text{Var}(\hat{T}_{lr} \mid S)$$

where $\mathcal{L}$ is the set of LR pairs of interest. This is a combinatorial optimization problem (NP-hard in general).

18.2 Greedy Approximation

Using the submodularity of $R^2_g$ as a function of the panel $S$ (adding a gene always reduces residual variance, but with diminishing returns), a greedy algorithm achieves a $(1 - 1/e) \approx 63\%$ approximation ratio:

Greedy Panel Design
  1. Initialize $S = \emptyset$
  2. For $j = 1, \dots, m$: $$g_j^* = \arg\max_{g \notin S} \sum_{(l,r) \in \mathcal{L}} \left[R_l^2(S \cup \{g\}) - R_l^2(S) + R_r^2(S \cup \{g\}) - R_r^2(S)\right]$$ $S \leftarrow S \cup \{g_j^*\}$

Fisher Information and Cramér-Rao Bounds

19.1 Fisher Information for Poisson

Definition 19.1

For $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, the Fisher information:

$$\mathcal{I}(\lambda) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \lambda} \log P(Y;\lambda)\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

Derivation:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \lambda} \log P(Y;\lambda) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \lambda}\left[Y \log \lambda - \lambda\right] = \frac{Y}{\lambda} - 1$$ $$\mathcal{I}(\lambda) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{Y}{\lambda} - 1\right)^2\right] = \frac{\text{Var}(Y)}{\lambda^2} = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

19.2 Cramér-Rao Lower Bound

Theorem 19.1 (Cramér-Rao Bound for Poisson)

For any unbiased estimator $\hat{\lambda}$ of $\lambda$ based on a single observation $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\lambda}) \geq \frac{1}{\mathcal{I}(\lambda)} = \lambda$$

For $N$ independent cells observing gene $g$ (all with the same rate $\lambda_g$):

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\lambda}_g) \geq \frac{\lambda_g}{N}$$
Remark 19.1

The Cramér-Rao bound for Poisson has a surprising implication: higher expression is harder to estimate precisely in absolute terms (variance = $\lambda/N$), but easier to estimate in relative terms (CV = $1/\sqrt{N\lambda}$). For CCI, where we compute products $\lambda_{il} \cdot \lambda_{jr}$, the absolute error matters more.

19.3 Fisher Information for the Rate Matrix

For the observed entries of $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$, the Fisher information matrix:

$$[\mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\Lambda})]_{(i,g),(i',g')} = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{\lambda_{ig}} & \text{if } (i,g) = (i',g') \text{ and } g \in S \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The total information about the rank-$K$ structure from $Nm$ observations:

$$\text{Total Fisher information} = \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{g \in S} \frac{1}{\lambda_{ig}}$$

Variance Estimation and Confidence Intervals

20.1 Variance of the CCI Statistic

For the true (non-imputed) CCI statistic with Poisson-distributed counts:

$$\text{Var}(T_{lr}) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|^2}\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}} \text{Var}(x_{il} \cdot x_{jr})$$

Since $x_{il}$ and $x_{jr}$ are independent (different cells):

$$\text{Var}(x_{il} \cdot x_{jr}) = \mathbb{E}[x_{il}^2]\mathbb{E}[x_{jr}^2] - (\mathbb{E}[x_{il}])^2(\mathbb{E}[x_{jr}])^2$$ $$= (\lambda_{il}^2 + \lambda_{il})(\lambda_{jr}^2 + \lambda_{jr}) - \lambda_{il}^2\lambda_{jr}^2$$ $$= \lambda_{il}^2\lambda_{jr} + \lambda_{il}\lambda_{jr}^2 + \lambda_{il}\lambda_{jr}$$

20.2 Delta Method for Transformed Statistics

For a function $h(\hat{T}_{lr})$ of the CCI statistic (e.g., log-transform for normalization):

$$\text{Var}(h(\hat{T}_{lr})) \approx [h'(T_{lr})]^2 \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{T}_{lr})$$

20.3 Confidence Intervals via Percentile Method

From the panel perturbation distribution $\{\hat{T}_{lr}^{(1)}, \dots, \hat{T}_{lr}^{(B)}\}$:

$$\text{CI}_{1-\alpha}(\hat{T}_{lr}) = \left[\hat{T}_{lr}^{(\lfloor B\alpha/2\rfloor)}, \; \hat{T}_{lr}^{(\lceil B(1-\alpha/2)\rceil)}\right]$$

where $\hat{T}_{lr}^{(k)}$ is the $k$-th order statistic. These are stability intervals, not confidence intervals with guaranteed coverage.

False Discovery Rate Control

21.1 Local FDR

The local false discovery rate for an LR pair with test statistic $T$:

$$\text{lfdr}(T) = \frac{\pi_0 f_0(T)}{f(T)}$$

where $\pi_0$ is the proportion of true nulls, $f_0$ is the null density, and $f$ is the mixture density.

21.2 Storey's Positive FDR

Definition 21.1
$$\text{pFDR}(t) = \frac{\pi_0 \cdot P_0(T \geq t)}{P(T \geq t)}$$

Estimated $\hat{\pi}_0$ via Storey's method:

$$\hat{\pi}_0 = \frac{\#\{i : p_i > \lambda\}}{L(1-\lambda)}$$

for a tuning parameter $\lambda \in (0,1)$ (typically $\lambda = 0.5$).

21.3 Impact of Imputation Bias on FDR

FDR Invalidation Under Biased Statistics

BH and Storey procedures assume valid $p$-values (uniform under the null). When CCI statistics are biased by imputation, the null $p$-values are stochastically smaller than uniform (anti-conservative). This means:

  • Nominal FDR control at 5% may produce actual FDR of 20–50%
  • Regression calibration restores validity by debiasing the test statistic
  • After correction, the permutation $p$-values should again be approximately uniform under the null
