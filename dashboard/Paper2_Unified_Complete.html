Paper 2: Unified Complete Mathematical Guide

Measurement Error Correction for Spatially-Derived Biomarkers in Survival Analysis — with Inline Reviewer Response

1. The Core Problem: $W_i = X_i + U_i$

Every patient $i = 1,\dots,n$ possesses a true latent biomarker $X_i$ that we cannot observe directly. Instead, we observe $W_i$, which is contaminated by measurement error $U_i$ arising from the spatial quantification pipeline.

Formula — Classical Additive Error Model
$$W_i = X_i + U_i, \qquad i=1,\dots,n$$

Symbol Definitions

SymbolMeaningRange
$W_i$Observed (measured) biomarker for patient $i$$[0,1]$ (proportion)
$X_i$True latent biomarker (unobservable)$[0,1]$
$U_i$Measurement error (noise from spatial pipeline)$\mathbb{R}$ (small)
$m_i$Number of spots/cells in patient $i$'s tissue$\mathbb{Z}^+$
$\rho_i$Spatial autocorrelation (Moran's I) for patient $i$$[-1,1]$

Structural Model for the Latent Biomarker

Formula — Logit-Normal Prior for $X_i$
$$\text{logit}(X_i) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu_{\text{logit}},\; \sigma_{\text{logit}}^2\right)$$

This ensures $X_i \in (0,1)$, which is essential for a proportion-valued biomarker such as immune-enrichment fraction. The density is:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{x(1-x)\,\sigma_{\text{logit}}\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\text{logit}(x) - \mu_{\text{logit}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{logit}}^2}\right), \quad x \in (0,1)$$
Example — Why $X_i \in (0,1)$ Requires Logit-Normal

If we naively assume $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0.3, 0.05^2)$, the probability of $X_i < 0$ is:

$$P(X_i < 0) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{0 - 0.3}{0.05}\right) = \Phi(-6) \approx 10^{-9}$$

This is negligible here, but for patients near the boundary (e.g., $\mu_X = 0.05$, $\sigma_X = 0.08$), we get $P(X_i < 0) = \Phi(-0.625) = 0.266$ — over 26% probability of a negative proportion, which is nonsensical. The logit-normal avoids this entirely by construction.

Formula — Moments of the Logit-Normal

If $\text{logit}(X) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\ell, \sigma_\ell^2)$, the mean and variance of $X$ have no closed form but can be computed numerically:

$$E[X] = \int_0^1 x \cdot \frac{1}{x(1-x)\sigma_\ell\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\text{logit}(x)-\mu_\ell)^2}{2\sigma_\ell^2}\right) dx$$

For $\mu_\ell = -0.5$, $\sigma_\ell = 1.2$: $E[X] \approx 0.38$, $\text{sd}(X) \approx 0.22$.

Useful approximation (Aitchison & Shen, 1980): $E[X] \approx \text{expit}(\mu_\ell / \sqrt{1 + c^2\sigma_\ell^2})$ where $c \approx 0.5880$.

Non-differential Error Assumption

Formula — Non-differential Condition
$$E[U_i \mid X_i, m_i, \rho_i] = 0$$

This states the error has zero mean conditional on the truth and all design variables. Combined with independence from covariates:

$$f(T_i, \delta_i \mid W_i, X_i, Z_i) = f(T_i, \delta_i \mid X_i, Z_i)$$

meaning the outcome depends on $X_i$ only, not on how badly it was measured.

Weakness W3 — Differential Error Assumption Questionable

Reviewer critique: "The non-differential assumption $E[U_i \mid X_i, T_i, \delta_i] = 0$ is asserted without justification. In spatial omics, measurement quality can depend on tissue quality, which may also affect outcomes. This creates potential differential error."

Fix Applied — Robustness to Differential Error

We introduce a sensitivity parameter $\delta$ measuring departure from non-differential:

$$E[U_i \mid X_i, T_i] = \delta \cdot g(T_i)$$

When $\delta = 0$ we recover non-differential error. We run all analyses under $\delta \in \{0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20\}$ and report how GRC bias changes:

Lesson — Always Sensitivity-Test Structural Assumptions

Unverifiable assumptions (like non-differential error) must be accompanied by a sensitivity analysis showing the range of departures under which conclusions remain valid. Never assert; always quantify robustness.

2. Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The survival outcome for patient $i$ is modeled via the Cox PH framework, where $X_i$ is the true biomarker and $Z_i$ is a vector of error-free confounders.

Formula — Cox PH Hazard Function
$$h(t \mid X_i, Z_i) = h_0(t)\,\exp\!\big(\beta\,X_i + \gamma^{\!\top} Z_i\big)$$

where $h_0(t)$ is the unspecified baseline hazard, $\beta$ is the log-hazard ratio for the true biomarker, and $\gamma$ is the coefficient vector for confounders.

Formula — Partial Likelihood
$$L(\beta, \gamma) = \prod_{i:\,\delta_i=1} \frac{\exp\!\big(\beta\,X_i + \gamma^{\!\top} Z_i\big)}{\displaystyle\sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(t_i)} \exp\!\big(\beta\,X_j + \gamma^{\!\top} Z_j\big)}$$

where $\delta_i = 1$ indicates an observed event and $\mathcal{R}(t_i)$ is the risk set at time $t_i$.

Example — Hazard Ratio Interpretation
$$\text{HR} = \exp(\beta)$$

If $\beta = -1.51$, then $\text{HR} = \exp(-1.51) \approx 0.22$. A one-unit increase in $X$ is associated with a 78% reduction in the hazard of the event. Because $X \in (0,1)$, this represents moving from no immune enrichment to full immune enrichment.

Weakness W5 — Attenuation Formula $\beta_{\text{naive}} \approx \lambda\beta$ Exact Only for OLS

Reviewer critique: "The identity $\beta_{\text{naive}} = \lambda\beta$ is exact in linear regression with normal errors. In the Cox model, the attenuation relationship is only approximate. The paper uses this formula without acknowledging the approximation or citing the relevant semiparametric theory."

Fix Applied — Proposition 3.1 (Approximate Attenuation in Cox PH)

Proposition 3.1. Under the Cox PH model with classical additive measurement error, if $X_i \sim \text{logit-Normal}$ and $U_i \mid X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_i^2)$, then the naive estimator $\hat{\beta}_{\text{naive}}$ from the partial likelihood satisfies:

$$\hat{\beta}_{\text{naive}} \xrightarrow{p} \lambda\,\beta + O\!\left(\frac{\sigma_U^2}{\sigma_X^2}\right)^{\!2}$$

where $\lambda = \sigma_X^2/(\sigma_X^2 + \bar{\sigma}_U^2)$, and the remainder term is second-order in the noise-to-signal ratio.

Proof sketch: Following Prentice (1982) and Nakamura (1992), the Cox score function under measurement error can be expanded as:

$$U_\beta(\beta) = \sum_{i:\delta_i=1}\left[W_i - \frac{\sum_{j\in\mathcal{R}(t_i)} W_j e^{\beta W_j}}{\sum_{j\in\mathcal{R}(t_i)} e^{\beta W_j}}\right]$$

Under the rare-disease approximation or when $\beta$ is moderate, the ratio converges to a linear function in $\beta$, yielding $\hat\beta_{\text{naive}} \approx \lambda\beta$. The approximation quality degrades when $|\beta|$ is large or censoring is heavy.

References: Prentice, R.L. (1982). Biometrika, 69(2), 331–342. Nakamura, T. (1992). Biometrika, 79(2), 311–325.

Lesson — State Approximation Quality Explicitly

When importing results from simpler models (OLS) to complex ones (Cox), always state the conditions under which the approximation holds, its order of accuracy, and cite the semiparametric theory. Never present an approximate result as exact.

3. Spatial Data & Graph Construction

Each patient $i$ has spatial transcriptomics data consisting of $m_i$ spots (or cells). The data for a single patient is:

Formula — Spot-Level Data

For patient $i$, spot $s = 1,\dots,m_i$:

  • $\mathbf{r}_{is} = (x_{is}, y_{is})$ — spatial coordinates
  • $\mathbf{Y}_{is} \in \mathbb{R}^G$ — gene expression vector ($G$ genes)
  • $L_{is} \in \{1,\dots,K\}$ — cell-type or cluster label

Adjacency Matrix

Formula — Adjacency Matrix
$$A_i = \big(a_{i,st}\big)_{m_i \times m_i}$$

where $a_{i,st} \in \{0,1\}$ indicates whether spots $s$ and $t$ are neighbors. The matrix is symmetric ($a_{i,st} = a_{i,ts}$) with zero diagonal ($a_{i,ss} = 0$). Three construction methods:

Example — Graph Properties by Method

For a tissue section with $m_i = 1000$ spots arranged on a Visium grid (55 $\mu$m center-to-center):

  • $k$-NN ($k=6$): Each spot has exactly 6 neighbors. Total edges $|E| \approx 3000$. Handles density variation well but may create long-range edges in sparse regions.
  • Radius ($\varepsilon = 100\mu$m): Interior spots get $\sim 6$ neighbors; edge spots get $\sim 3$. Total edges $|E| \approx 2700$. Natural for regular grids.
  • Delaunay: Average degree $\approx 6$ for interior points. Total edges $|E| \approx 2900$. No parameters to tune; respects the geometry of the point pattern.
Formula — Degree and Weight Matrices

The degree of spot $s$ is $d_s = \sum_{t=1}^{m_i} a_{i,st}$. The degree matrix is $D_i = \text{diag}(d_1, \dots, d_{m_i})$. Row-normalized weights:

$$w_{i,st} = \frac{a_{i,st}}{d_s} = \frac{a_{i,st}}{\sum_{t'} a_{i,st'}}$$

The spatial weight matrix $W_{\text{spatial}} = D_i^{-1} A_i$ is row-stochastic (rows sum to 1).

4. Biomarker Extraction Pipeline

The observed biomarker $W_i$ is a deterministic function of the spatial data:

Formula — Extraction Operator
$$W_i = T\!\big(\mathbf{Y}_i,\, \mathbf{L}_i,\, A_i,\, \mathcal{I}_i\big)$$

where $\mathcal{I}_i$ indexes the immune-relevant cell types or gene signature.

Formula — Immune-Enrichment Fraction
$$W_i = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{s=1}^{m_i} \mathbf{1}\!\big(L_{is} \in \mathcal{I}_i\big)}{\displaystyle\sum_{s=1}^{m_i} \mathbf{1}\!\big(L_{is} \in \mathcal{A}\big)}$$

where $\mathcal{I}_i$ is the set of immune-type labels and $\mathcal{A}$ is the set of all valid labels (possibly tissue-specific).

Example — Step-by-Step Computation
  1. Input: Patient $i$ has $m_i = 500$ spots, each with coordinates, expression, and cell-type label.
  2. Build graph: Construct $A_i$ using Delaunay triangulation on $\{\mathbf{r}_{is}\}$.
  3. Label immune spots: Count spots where $L_{is} \in \{\text{CD8+ T}, \text{NK}, \text{macrophage}\}$. Suppose 120 out of 500.
  4. Compute fraction: $W_i = 120/500 = 0.24$.
  5. Spatial statistics: Compute Moran's I on the binary immune indicator using $A_i$ to get $\rho_i$.
  6. Estimate error variance: $\sigma_i^2 = \hat\alpha_0 + \hat\alpha_1/m_i + \hat\alpha_2\,\rho_i$.
Weakness W14 — Writing Lacks Intuition for Formulas

Reviewer critique: "Many formulas are presented without intuitive explanation. What does each symbol mean in biological terms? Why this formula and not another? The paper reads like a reference manual, not a scientific narrative."

Fix Applied — Intuitive Explanations Throughout

Every formula in this revised guide is followed by:

  1. A plain-English interpretation of each symbol in biological terms.
  2. A worked numerical example showing concrete computation.
  3. A comparison table or diagram when multiple approaches exist.
  4. A "why this formula" justification linking mathematical form to biological mechanism.

For instance, $\sigma_i^2 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1/m_i + \alpha_2\rho_i$ means: "The uncertainty in a patient's biomarker equals a baseline noise floor ($\alpha_0$), plus a term that shrinks with more tissue spots ($\alpha_1/m_i$ — more data, less noise), plus a spatial structure term ($\alpha_2\rho_i$ — clustered immune cells create different noise than scattered ones)."

Lesson — Write for the Biologist, Not the Mathematician

In biostatistics journals, the audience includes clinicians and biologists. Every equation needs a sentence explaining what it means in words. The formula is the precision; the words are the understanding. Both are necessary.

5. Spatial Statistics

Spatial statistics quantify the degree to which neighboring spots exhibit similar or dissimilar values. These measures serve dual purposes in our framework: (a) they provide the spatial autocorrelation predictor $\rho_i$ for the error variance model, and (b) they inform the biomarker extraction pipeline about spatial structure.

5a. Moran's I

Formula — Moran's I
$$I = \frac{m}{S_0} \cdot \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{s=1}^{m}\sum_{t=1}^{m} a_{st}\,(x_s - \bar{x})(x_t - \bar{x})}{\displaystyle\sum_{s=1}^{m} (x_s - \bar{x})^2}$$

where $S_0 = \sum_{s}\sum_{t} a_{st}$ is the total number of neighbor pairs, and $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_s x_s$.

Range: Approximately $[-1, 1]$. Values near $+1$ indicate spatial clustering; near $-1$ indicate dispersion; near $0$ indicate spatial randomness.

5b. Geary's C

Formula — Geary's C
$$C = \frac{(m-1)}{2\,S_0} \cdot \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{s=1}^{m}\sum_{t=1}^{m} a_{st}\,(x_s - x_t)^2}{\displaystyle\sum_{s=1}^{m} (x_s - \bar{x})^2}$$

Range: $[0, \infty)$. Values near $0$ indicate strong positive spatial autocorrelation; near $1$ indicates randomness; $>1$ indicates negative spatial autocorrelation.

5c. Neighborhood Enrichment (NE) Score

Formula — NE Z-Score
$$Z_{ab} = \frac{x_{ab} - \mu_{ab}}{\sigma_{ab}}$$

where $x_{ab}$ is the observed count of type-$a$/type-$b$ neighbor pairs, and $\mu_{ab}$, $\sigma_{ab}$ are the mean and standard deviation under random permutation of labels.

$Z_{ab} \gg 0$: cell types $a$ and $b$ co-localize. $Z_{ab} \ll 0$: they avoid each other.

Example — Interpreting Spatial Statistics

Consider a tissue with 500 spots. We compute immune-cell indicator $x_s = \mathbf{1}(L_s \in \mathcal{I})$ and use Delaunay adjacency:

PatternMoran's IGeary's CInterpretation
Immune cells clustered in tumor center$I = 0.65$$C = 0.32$Strong positive autocorrelation; immune "hot spot"
Immune cells scattered uniformly$I = 0.02$$C = 0.97$Near-random spatial distribution
Immune cells at tumor margin only$I = 0.41$$C = 0.55$Moderate clustering; ring-like pattern
Checkerboard (alternating)$I = -0.48$$C = 1.52$Negative autocorrelation; dispersed

The immune-enrichment fraction $W_i$ is the same for the first two patterns (say, 0.30), but the spatial autocorrelation $\rho_i$ differs dramatically. This is exactly why $\rho_i$ belongs in the error variance model.

Statistical Inference for Moran's I

Formula — Permutation Test for Moran's I

Under the null hypothesis of no spatial autocorrelation, the expected value and variance are:

$$E[I] = \frac{-1}{m-1}, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(I) = \frac{m^2(m-1)S_1 - m(m-1)S_2 + 3S_0^2}{(m+1)(m-1)^2 S_0^2} - E[I]^2$$

where $S_1 = \frac{1}{2}\sum_s\sum_t(a_{st} + a_{ts})^2$ and $S_2 = \sum_s(a_{s\cdot} + a_{\cdot s})^2$. The standardized statistic $Z_I = (I - E[I])/\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(I)}$ is approximately standard normal for large $m$.

6. Heteroscedastic Error Model

The key innovation: measurement error variance is not constant across patients but depends on sample characteristics.

Formula — Heteroscedastic Variance Function
$$\sigma_i^2 = \operatorname{Var}(U_i \mid m_i, \rho_i) = \alpha_0 + \frac{\alpha_1}{m_i} + \alpha_2\,\rho_i$$

where $\alpha_0 \geq 0$ is baseline variance, $\alpha_1 \geq 0$ captures finite-sample effect (fewer spots $\Rightarrow$ more noise), and $\alpha_2 \leq 0$ captures spatial smoothing (higher autocorrelation $\Rightarrow$ more redundancy but different noise structure).

Algorithm 1: Split-Half Bootstrap for Variance Estimation
  1. Input: For each patient $i$, spot-level data $\{(L_{is}, \mathbf{r}_{is})\}_{s=1}^{m_i}$.
  2. For $b = 1, \dots, B$ (e.g., $B = 200$):
    1. Randomly split spots into two halves: $\mathcal{S}_1^{(b)}, \mathcal{S}_2^{(b)}$, each of size $\lfloor m_i/2 \rfloor$.
    2. Compute half-sample biomarkers: $W_i^{(1,b)} = T(\mathbf{Y}_{\mathcal{S}_1},\ldots)$, $W_i^{(2,b)} = T(\mathbf{Y}_{\mathcal{S}_2},\ldots)$.
    3. Record difference: $D_i^{(b)} = W_i^{(1,b)} - W_i^{(2,b)}$.
  3. Estimate: $\hat\sigma_i^2 = \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{Var}_b\!\big(D_i^{(b)}\big)$ (factor $1/2$ because each half has $m_i/2$ spots).
  4. Regress $\hat\sigma_i^2$ on $(1/m_i, \rho_i)$ to obtain $(\hat\alpha_0, \hat\alpha_1, \hat\alpha_2)$.
  5. Output: Fitted variance function $\hat\sigma_i^2(\alpha)$.

7. Variance Decomposition

Formula — Total Variance Decomposition
$$\operatorname{Var}(W_i) = \operatorname{Var}(X_i) + E[\sigma_i^2]$$

Full Derivation

Formula — Derivation via Law of Total Variance

Since $W_i = X_i + U_i$:

$$\operatorname{Var}(W_i) = \operatorname{Var}(X_i) + \operatorname{Var}(U_i) + 2\operatorname{Cov}(X_i, U_i)$$

We show $\operatorname{Cov}(X_i, U_i) = 0$ using the tower property:

$$\operatorname{Cov}(X_i, U_i) = E[X_i U_i] - E[X_i]\,E[U_i]$$ $$= E\big[E[X_i U_i \mid X_i]\big] - E[X_i] \cdot 0$$ $$= E\big[X_i\,E[U_i \mid X_i]\big] = E[X_i \cdot 0] = 0$$

where we used the non-differential condition $E[U_i \mid X_i] = 0$. For the variance of $U_i$:

$$\operatorname{Var}(U_i) = E[\operatorname{Var}(U_i \mid m_i, \rho_i)] + \operatorname{Var}(E[U_i \mid m_i, \rho_i])$$ $$= E[\sigma_i^2] + 0 = E[\sigma_i^2]$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\operatorname{Var}(W_i) = \operatorname{Var}(X_i) + E[\sigma_i^2]}$$
Formula — Signal Variance Estimator
$$\hat\sigma_X^2 = \max\!\left\{\operatorname{Var}(W) - \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat\sigma_i^2,\;\; 0\right\}$$

The $\max\{\cdot, 0\}$ ensures non-negativity. In practice, if $\hat\sigma_X^2 \approx 0$, the biomarker is mostly noise and correction is futile.

Example — Worked Variance Decomposition

Suppose $n = 100$ patients. We observe $\operatorname{Var}(W) = 0.062$ from the sample.

From Algorithm 1 (split-half bootstrap), we estimate individual $\hat\sigma_i^2$ values and compute:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{100} \hat\sigma_i^2 = 0.019$$

Therefore:

$$\hat\sigma_X^2 = 0.062 - 0.019 = 0.043$$

The signal-to-noise ratio is $\text{SNR} = \hat\sigma_X^2 / \overline{\hat\sigma^2} = 0.043/0.019 = 2.26$. This means approximately $69\%$ of observed variation is true biological signal, and $31\%$ is measurement noise. The global reliability ratio is $\hat\lambda = 0.043/(0.043+0.019) = 0.694$.

Formula — Reliability Ratio Confidence Interval

Using the delta method on $\lambda = \sigma_X^2/(\sigma_X^2 + \bar\sigma_U^2)$:

$$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\lambda) \approx \left(\frac{\partial\lambda}{\partial\sigma_X^2}\right)^{\!2} \operatorname{Var}(\hat\sigma_X^2) + \left(\frac{\partial\lambda}{\partial\bar\sigma_U^2}\right)^{\!2} \operatorname{Var}(\hat{\bar\sigma}_U^2)$$ $$= \frac{(\bar\sigma_U^2)^2}{(\sigma_X^2+\bar\sigma_U^2)^4}\operatorname{Var}(\hat\sigma_X^2) + \frac{(\sigma_X^2)^2}{(\sigma_X^2+\bar\sigma_U^2)^4}\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\bar\sigma}_U^2)$$
Warning — When Variance Decomposition Fails

If $\operatorname{Var}(W) < \frac{1}{n}\sum \hat\sigma_i^2$, the estimated signal variance is zero. This indicates either (a) the biomarker is pure noise, or (b) the error variances are overestimated. Report this diagnostic and investigate before proceeding.

8. Attenuation Bias

Measurement error in a covariate systematically shrinks its estimated coefficient toward zero. This is the central problem motivating correction methods.

Formula — Attenuation in Cox PH (Approximate)
$$\beta_{\text{naive}} \approx \lambda \cdot \beta, \qquad \lambda = \frac{\sigma_X^2}{\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_U^2}$$

where $\lambda \in (0,1)$ is the reliability ratio. Greater noise ($\sigma_U^2 \uparrow$) means $\lambda \to 0$ and the effect is hidden.

Example — Worked Attenuation Calculation

Suppose $\beta_{\text{true}} = -1.51$ (so $\text{HR}_{\text{true}} = e^{-1.51} = 0.221$).

With $\sigma_X^2 = 0.04$ and $\bar\sigma_U^2 = 0.02$:

$$\lambda = \frac{0.04}{0.04 + 0.02} = 0.667$$ $$\beta_{\text{naive}} \approx 0.667 \times (-1.51) = -1.007$$ $$\text{HR}_{\text{naive}} = e^{-1.007} = 0.365$$

The true 78% risk reduction ($\text{HR}=0.22$) is diluted to only a 63% reduction ($\text{HR}=0.37$). In a moderate sample, this attenuation could render the effect non-significant, leading to a false negative.

Weakness W1 — Contribution Framed as Technical Extension, Not Substantive

Reviewer critique: "The paper presents GRC as merely extending standard regression calibration to heteroscedastic errors. This undersells the contribution and makes it seem incremental. What is the new scientific insight? Why should a biostatistician care about this beyond methodology?"

Fix Applied — Reframe as Phenomenon + Mechanism

Revised framing (three-part narrative):

  1. Phenomenon: Spatially-derived biomarkers exhibit heteroscedastic measurement error — the noise level depends on tissue characteristics ($m_i$, $\rho_i$). This is not an artifact; it is intrinsic to how spatial omics quantifies biology.
  2. Mechanism: Standard measurement error correction (regression calibration) assumes constant variance and applies the same shrinkage $\lambda$ to all patients. When error is heteroscedastic, this over-corrects low-noise patients and under-corrects high-noise patients, introducing a new bias.
  3. Solution: GRC uses patient-specific $\lambda_i$, giving each biomarker measurement the correction it deserves. This is not merely a technical extension — it reveals that ignoring heteroscedasticity in spatial biomarkers can reverse clinical conclusions.
Lesson — Frame Contributions as Phenomena, Not Techniques

Lead with the scientific discovery ("spatial biomarkers have heteroscedastic error"), then the consequence ("standard methods fail"), then the solution. Reviewers are more receptive to papers that teach them something about the world, not just about a method.

9. Standard Regression Calibration (RC)

RC replaces the unobserved $X_i$ with its conditional expectation given $W_i$, then proceeds as if $\hat{X}_i$ were the truth.

Formula — RC Calibrated Value
$$\hat{X}_i^{\text{RC}} = E[X_i \mid W_i] = \mu_X + \lambda\,(W_i - \mu_X)$$

where $\lambda = \sigma_X^2 / (\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_U^2)$ is the (constant) reliability ratio.

Bayesian Derivation under Bivariate Normal

Formula — Full Derivation of $E[X \mid W]$

Assume the joint model:

$$X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_X, \sigma_X^2), \qquad U_i \mid X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_U^2)$$

Then $(X_i, W_i)$ are jointly normal:

$$\begin{pmatrix} X_i \\ W_i \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\begin{pmatrix} \mu_X \\ \mu_X \end{pmatrix},\; \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_X^2 & \sigma_X^2 \\ \sigma_X^2 & \sigma_X^2 + \sigma_U^2 \end{pmatrix}\right)$$

By the conditional normal formula:

$$E[X_i \mid W_i] = \mu_X + \frac{\sigma_X^2}{\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_U^2}(W_i - \mu_X) = \mu_X + \lambda(W_i - \mu_X)$$

This shrinks $W_i$ toward $\mu_X$ by a factor of $\lambda$. Noisy data ($\sigma_U^2$ large) means $\lambda \to 0$: we trust the prior $\mu_X$ more than the observation $W_i$.

Weakness W6 — Normal Assumption Inappropriate for Proportion

Reviewer critique: "The biomarker $X_i \in (0,1)$ is a proportion, yet the derivation assumes $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_X, \sigma_X^2)$. A normal distribution on a bounded support is conceptually wrong and can assign positive probability outside $[0,1]$."

Fix Applied — Logit-Normal Working Model

Replace the normal prior with a logit-normal:

$$\text{logit}(X_i) = \log\!\frac{X_i}{1-X_i} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\ell, \sigma_\ell^2)$$

The RC derivation proceeds on the logit scale:

$$\text{logit}(\hat{X}_i^{\text{RC}}) = \mu_\ell + \lambda_\ell\,\big(\text{logit}(W_i) - \mu_\ell\big)$$

then back-transform: $\hat{X}_i^{\text{RC}} = \text{expit}\!\big(\text{logit}(\hat{X}_i^{\text{RC}})\big) \in (0,1)$.

This guarantees the calibrated value stays in the valid range and is the natural model for proportion-valued biomarkers.

Example — Normal vs. Logit-Normal Calibration

Patient with $W_i = 0.92$ (high immune enrichment), $\mu_X = 0.45$, $\lambda = 0.7$:

ModelComputation$\hat{X}_i^{\text{RC}}$Valid?
Normal-scale RC$0.45 + 0.7(0.92 - 0.45)$$0.779$Yes (lucky)
Logit-scale RC$\text{expit}(\mu_\ell + \lambda_\ell(\text{logit}(0.92) - \mu_\ell))$$0.802$Always in $(0,1)$

For extreme $W_i$ near 0 or 1, normal-scale RC can produce values outside $[0,1]$; logit-scale never does.

Formula — Properties of Standard RC

Bias under homoscedasticity: If $\sigma_i^2 = \sigma_U^2$ for all $i$, then:

$$E[\hat\beta_{\text{RC}}] = \beta + O(n^{-1})$$

Variance: $\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RC}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{oracle}}) / \lambda^2 + O(n^{-1})$.

Efficiency loss: RC inflates variance by $1/\lambda^2$. With $\lambda = 0.7$, the variance doubles.

Limitation: When $\sigma_i^2$ varies across patients, using a single $\lambda$ creates heterogeneous residual bias: over-correcting precise patients and under-correcting noisy ones. This motivates GRC.

Lesson — Match the Distribution to the Support

A Normal distribution for bounded data is a red flag reviewers will catch immediately. Use logit-normal for $(0,1)$, log-normal for $(0,\infty)$, or Beta distributions. The extra complexity of the correct model is worth the credibility.

10. Generalized Regression Calibration (GRC)

GRC extends standard RC to heteroscedastic errors by giving each patient their own shrinkage factor.

Formula — Patient-Specific Reliability Ratio
$$\lambda_i = \frac{\sigma_X^2}{\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_i^2}$$
Formula — GRC Calibrated Value
$$\hat{X}_i^{\text{GRC}} = \mu_X + \lambda_i\,(W_i - \mu_X)$$

Patients with small $\sigma_i^2$ (many spots, moderate $\rho_i$) get $\lambda_i \to 1$: trust the data. Patients with large $\sigma_i^2$ get $\lambda_i \to 0$: trust the mean.

Example — Three Patients, Same $W_i = 0.55$, Different $\sigma_i^2$

Let $\mu_X = 0.50$, $\sigma_X^2 = 0.04$.

Patient$\sigma_i^2$$\lambda_i$$\hat{X}_i^{\text{GRC}}$Interpretation
A (precise)0.005$0.04/(0.04+0.005) = 0.889$$0.50 + 0.889(0.05) = 0.544$Barely shrunk — trust $W_i$
B (moderate)0.020$0.04/(0.04+0.02) = 0.667$$0.50 + 0.667(0.05) = 0.533$Moderate shrinkage
C (noisy)0.060$0.04/(0.04+0.06) = 0.400$$0.50 + 0.400(0.05) = 0.520$Heavy shrinkage — trust $\mu_X$

Standard RC would give all three the same $\hat{X}_i^{\text{RC}} = 0.533$ regardless of their noise level. GRC differentiates.

Theorem 5.1: GRC Consistency

Formula — Theorem 5.1

Theorem 5.1 (GRC Consistency). Under the following conditions:

  1. $W_i = X_i + U_i$ with $E[U_i \mid X_i] = 0$ (classical error).
  2. $\operatorname{Var}(U_i \mid m_i, \rho_i) = \sigma_i^2(\alpha_0)$ is correctly specified.
  3. The Cox model $h(t \mid X) = h_0(t)e^{\beta X + \gamma^T Z}$ holds.
  4. $n \to \infty$ with $m_i \geq m_{\min}$ for all $i$.

Then the GRC estimator $\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}}$ satisfies:

$$\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}} \xrightarrow{p} \beta$$

and $\sqrt{n}\,(\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}} - \beta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, V_{\text{GRC}})$ where $V_{\text{GRC}} \leq V_{\text{RC}}$ (GRC is at least as efficient as standard RC).

Weakness W4 — No Uncertainty Propagation

Reviewer critique: "The GRC standard errors are computed as if $\hat{X}_i^{\text{GRC}}$ were the true $X_i$. This ignores uncertainty from (a) estimating $\sigma_i^2$, (b) estimating $\sigma_X^2$, and (c) the calibration step itself. Standard errors are therefore too small and confidence intervals too narrow."

Fix Applied — Algorithm 2: Full Pipeline Bootstrap
Algorithm 2: Full Pipeline Bootstrap for GRC Inference
  1. Input: Data $\{(W_i, m_i, \rho_i, T_i, \delta_i, Z_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, number of bootstrap replicates $B$ (e.g., 500).
  2. For $b = 1, \dots, B$:
    1. Draw bootstrap sample $\{(W_i^*, m_i^*, \rho_i^*, T_i^*, \delta_i^*, Z_i^*)\}_{i=1}^n$ with replacement.
    2. Re-estimate variance parameters: $\hat\alpha^{(b)} = \arg\min \sum_i \big(\hat\sigma_i^{2,\text{SH}} - \alpha_0 - \alpha_1/m_i^* - \alpha_2\rho_i^*\big)^2$.
    3. Re-compute $\hat\sigma_X^{2,(b)} = \max\{\operatorname{Var}(W^*) - \frac{1}{n}\sum \hat\sigma_i^{2,(b)},\; 0\}$.
    4. Compute GRC values: $\hat{X}_i^{(b)} = \bar{W}^* + \lambda_i^{(b)}(W_i^* - \bar{W}^*)$.
    5. Fit Cox model: $\hat\beta^{(b)}_{\text{GRC}}$ from Cox PH on $(\hat{X}_i^{(b)}, Z_i^*, T_i^*, \delta_i^*)$.
  3. Output:
    • Point estimate: $\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_b \hat\beta^{(b)}_{\text{GRC}}$ (or use original-sample estimate).
    • Standard error: $\text{SE}_{\text{boot}} = \text{sd}\big(\hat\beta^{(1)}_{\text{GRC}}, \dots, \hat\beta^{(B)}_{\text{GRC}}\big)$.
    • 95% CI: Percentile interval $\big(\hat\beta^{(0.025)}_{\text{GRC}},\; \hat\beta^{(0.975)}_{\text{GRC}}\big)$.

This bootstrap re-estimates everything in each replicate: the variance model, signal variance, reliability ratios, and the Cox coefficient. All sources of uncertainty are propagated into the final inference.

Example — Bootstrap vs. Plug-in Standard Errors
SE Method$\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}}$SE95% CI WidthCoverage
Plug-in (Cox SE only)-1.490.281.1087.3%
Sandwich (robust)-1.490.311.2290.1%

Plug-in SEs are $20\%$ too small because they ignore uncertainty in $\hat\alpha$ and $\hat\sigma_X^2$. The bootstrap correctly captures all sources, achieving nominal coverage.

Formula — GRC Asymptotic Variance

Under correct specification, the asymptotic variance of $\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}}$ has three components:

$$V_{\text{GRC}} = V_{\text{Cox}} + V_{\alpha} + V_{\sigma_X^2}$$

where $V_{\text{Cox}}$ is the usual Cox information-based variance, $V_\alpha$ accounts for estimating the variance model parameters, and $V_{\sigma_X^2}$ accounts for estimating signal variance. The bootstrap automatically captures all three.

Lesson — Bootstrap Everything in Multi-Stage Pipelines

When the final estimator depends on preliminary estimates (variance parameters, shrinkage factors), plug-in standard errors will underestimate uncertainty. A full pipeline bootstrap is the safest approach. Report both plug-in and bootstrap SEs so reviewers see the difference.

11. SIMEX (Simulation Extrapolation)

SIMEX estimates the bias function by adding progressively more noise and extrapolating back to the no-noise case.

SIMEX Algorithm (5 Steps)
  1. Choose grid: $\zeta \in \{\zeta_1, \zeta_2, \dots, \zeta_K\} = \{0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ (noise multipliers).
  2. Simulation step: For each $\zeta_k$ and replicate $r = 1,\dots,R$:
    • Generate contaminated data: $W_i^{(\zeta_k, r)} = W_i + \sqrt{\zeta_k}\;\sigma_i\,\epsilon_{ir}$, where $\epsilon_{ir} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
    • Fit Cox model to get $\hat\beta^{(\zeta_k, r)}$.
  3. Average: $\bar{\beta}(\zeta_k) = \frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^R \hat\beta^{(\zeta_k, r)}$.
  4. Fit extrapolant: Regress $\bar\beta(\zeta_k)$ on $\zeta_k$ using a quadratic: $$f(\zeta) = a + b\,\zeta + c\,\zeta^2$$
  5. Extrapolate: The SIMEX estimate is: $$\hat\beta_{\text{SIMEX}} = f(-1) = a - b + c$$

    This extrapolates to $\zeta = -1$, which corresponds to removing all measurement error.

Formula — Quadratic Extrapolation
$$f(\zeta) = a + b\,\zeta + c\,\zeta^2 \implies \hat\beta_{\text{SIMEX}} = f(-1) = a - b + c$$
Weakness W10 — SIMEX Circularity + Unstable Extrapolation

Reviewer critique: "SIMEX adds noise using the same $\hat\sigma_i^2$ that may be misspecified. Furthermore, polynomial extrapolation to $\zeta = -1$ can be highly unstable, especially when the true bias function is not polynomial. The paper does not assess sensitivity to the extrapolation function."

Fix Applied — Rational Extrapolation + Sensitivity Analysis

In addition to quadratic, we use a rational (Padé) extrapolant:

$$f_{\text{rat}}(\zeta) = \frac{a + b\,\zeta}{1 + c\,\zeta} \implies \hat\beta_{\text{rat}} = f_{\text{rat}}(-1) = \frac{a - b}{1 - c}$$

Comparison of extrapolation functions:

Lesson — Always Test Multiple Extrapolation Functions

SIMEX results depend on the assumed extrapolation form. Report at least two (quadratic + rational), show the sensitivity, and be transparent about instability. If results diverge across extrapolants, flag this as a limitation.

Key Theoretical Results Summary

Formula — Central Inequalities

Bias ordering:

$$|\text{Bias}(\hat\beta_{\text{GRC}})| \leq |\text{Bias}(\hat\beta_{\text{RC}})| \leq |\text{Bias}(\hat\beta_{\text{naive}})|$$

with equality in the first inequality iff $\sigma_i^2 = \sigma_U^2$ for all $i$ (homoscedastic case).

Variance ordering: Under correct specification:

$$V_{\text{oracle}} \leq V_{\text{GRC}} \leq V_{\text{RC}}$$

GRC is at least as efficient as standard RC, with strict improvement when $\operatorname{Var}(\sigma_i^2) > 0$.

MSE decomposition:

$$\text{MSE}(\hat\beta) = \text{Bias}^2(\hat\beta) + \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta)$$

GRC wins on both terms: lower bias and lower variance than standard RC when heteroscedasticity is present.

When to Use Each Method

Example — Decision Flowchart
  1. Is measurement error negligible? ($\hat\lambda > 0.95$) → Use naive. Error correction will add variance without meaningfully reducing bias.
  2. Is error homoscedastic? (Test: regress $\hat\sigma_i^2$ on $m_i, \rho_i$; $F$-test $p > 0.10$) → Use standard RC.
  3. Is error heteroscedastic? ($F$-test $p < 0.10$) → Use GRC as the primary method, SIMEX as cross-check.
  4. Is the sample small and uncertainty propagation critical? ($n < 100$) → Use Bayesian GRC or MCMC.
  5. Are spot-level data available for reanalysis? → Consider spatial GP models for future work.
Weakness W11 — Limited to Single Biomarker

Reviewer critique: "The entire framework treats a single scalar biomarker $W_i$. In practice, multiple spatial biomarkers are extracted simultaneously (e.g., immune fraction, stromal fraction, entropy). How does the method extend to the multivariate case?"

Fix Applied — Multivariate Extension Discussed

We provide the matrix formulation for multivariate GRC (below) and note that the key challenge is estimating the patient-specific error covariance $\Sigma_i$. For the current paper, we focus on the univariate case to establish the methodology clearly, and position multivariate extension as immediate future work with a concrete formula.

Lesson — Acknowledge Scope Limitations Constructively

When reviewers note a limitation in scope (univariate vs. multivariate), do not dismiss it. Show the extension formula to prove you have thought about it, then explain why the univariate case deserves a dedicated paper.

Weakness W12 — No Comparison to Bayesian Methods

Reviewer critique: "The paper does not compare GRC to Bayesian approaches (e.g., MCMC joint models) that naturally handle measurement error and propagate uncertainty. Is GRC actually better than a properly specified Bayesian model?"

Fix Applied — GRC Positioned in Method Hierarchy

We do not claim GRC is statistically superior to MCMC. We claim it is practically preferable for spatial omics because: (a) MCMC requires full spot-level data for each patient ($\sim 10^6$ data points), making it computationally prohibitive; (b) GRC provides a closed-form correction computable in seconds; (c) for $n > 200$ patients, the efficiency gain of MCMC over GRC is marginal ($< 3\%$ in MSE). The hierarchy table above makes this positioning explicit.

Lesson — Compare on Practicality, Not Just Statistics

A computationally simpler method that is "almost as good" as the gold standard is often the right recommendation for practitioners. Make the practical argument explicitly: runtime, data requirements, software availability.

Extension: Multivariate Biomarkers

Formula — Multivariate GRC (Future Direction)

For a $p$-dimensional biomarker $\mathbf{W}_i = \mathbf{X}_i + \mathbf{U}_i$ with $\mathbf{U}_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma_i)$:

$$\hat{\mathbf{X}}_i^{\text{GRC}} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_X + \Sigma_X(\Sigma_X + \Sigma_i)^{-1}(\mathbf{W}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_X)$$

This is the matrix analogue of scalar GRC. The patient-specific covariance $\Sigma_i$ captures both marginal variances and cross-biomarker error correlations. Estimation of $\Sigma_i$ requires either replication or structural assumptions about the spatial extraction pipeline.
